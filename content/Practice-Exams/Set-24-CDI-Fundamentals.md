### 1. What is the primary and overarching objective of a criminal investigation?

- [ ] To secure a conviction by any means necessary.
- [x] To legally obtain truth, identify, and prosecute the perpetrator.
- [ ] To recover all stolen property and assets.
- [ ] To satisfy the public's demand for justice.

> **Explanation:** The fundamental goal of criminal investigation is to uncover the truth about a crime. This involves legally gathering facts to identify and locate the guilty party and providing sufficient evidence to secure a conviction in a court of law. It is not merely about conviction but about the legal and truthful process.

### 2. Which of the "three I's" or tools of investigation refers to the scientific examination of physical evidence?

- [ ] Information
- [ ] Interrogation
- [x] Instrumentation
- [ ] Intuition

> **Explanation:** Instrumentation refers to the use of scientific instruments and methods in the examination of physical evidence. This is a critical component of modern criminalistics, involving forensic chemistry, physics, and biology to analyze clues. Information is the raw data gathered, while Interrogation involves questioning subjects.

### 3. An investigator at a crime scene is trying to establish the sequence of events. Which of the cardinal points of investigation is being addressed?

- [ ] What specific offense has been committed?
- [ ] Who committed the crime?
- [x] How was the crime committed?
- [ ] Where was the crime committed?

> **Explanation:** The question "How was the crime committed?" (How) seeks to determine the modus operandi or the method and sequence of actions the perpetrator took to commit the crime. This helps in reconstructing the event and understanding the offender's behavior. The other options refer to What, Who, and Where.

### 4. A bloody knife found at a murder scene is best classified as what type of evidence?

- [ ] Testimonial Evidence
- [ ] Documentary Evidence
- [x] Physical Evidence
- [ ] Hearsay Evidence

> **Explanation:** Physical evidence (also known as real or object evidence) consists of tangible articles that can prove or disprove a fact. A bloody knife is a classic example, as it can be scientifically examined for fingerprints, DNA, and trace evidence, directly linking it to the crime or a person.

### 5. What is the first and most critical step a first responder must take upon arriving at a crime scene?

- [ ] Immediately start collecting evidence.
- [ ] Chase down any potential suspects seen leaving the area.
- [x] Secure and protect the scene, preserving its integrity.
- [ ] Interview all witnesses present.

> **Explanation:** The initial and most crucial duty of a first responder is to secure and protect the crime scene. This involves establishing a perimeter and controlling access to prevent the contamination, alteration, or destruction of evidence. All other actions, like evidence collection and witness interviews, follow after the scene is properly secured.

### 6. An investigator uses a method where the search starts from a central point and moves outward in ever-larger circles. What search method is this?

- [ ] Grid Method
- [x] Spiral Method
- [ ] Strip Method
- [ ] Zone Method

> **Explanation:** The spiral method involves searching in a circular pattern, either from the outside in or from the inside out. It is often used by a single investigator in outdoor scenes or rooms with no significant physical barriers.

### 7. What is the primary purpose of maintaining a "chain of custody" for evidence?

- [ ] To determine the monetary value of the evidence.
- [ ] To create a list of all investigators involved in the case.
- [x] To ensure the integrity and admissibility of evidence in court.
- [ ] To track the movement of suspects related to the evidence.

> **Explanation:** The chain of custody is the chronological documentation of the seizure, control, transfer, analysis, and disposition of evidence. Its purpose is to prove that the evidence presented in court is the same as that which was collected at the scene, and that it has remained untampered, thus ensuring its integrity and admissibility.

### 8. The consistent and repeated pattern of behavior or method used by a criminal when committing multiple crimes is known as what?

- [ ] Criminal Signature
- [x] Modus Operandi (MO)
- [ ] Profiling
- [ ] Case Linkage

> **Explanation:** Modus Operandi (MO) refers to a criminal's characteristic way of committing a crime. It includes the tools they use, the time and location they prefer, and the general steps they take. While related to a signature (which satisfies a psychological need), the MO is about the practical mechanics of the crime.

### 9. Which of the following is a fundamental responsibility of an investigator at the crime scene?

- [ ] To declare the suspect's guilt to the media.
- [x] To conduct a careful and methodical assessment of the scene.
- [ ] To perform the autopsy on the victim.
- [ ] To act as the prosecutor during the trial.

> **Explanation:** An investigator's duty at the scene is to perform a systematic assessment. This includes observing, documenting (notes, photos, sketches), and searching for evidence. This methodical approach ensures that no crucial details are missed and that the scene is processed professionally.

### 11. Which phase of investigation involves collecting raw data, including witness statements, records, and observations?

- [x] Information gathering
- [ ] Interrogation and Interview
- [ ] Instrumentation analysis
- [ ] Case preparation

> **Explanation:** The "Information" phase is the foundation of an investigation. It is the process of gathering all possible raw data, facts, and details about the crime from various sources like records, reports, and initial witness accounts before proceeding to more focused methods like interrogation.

### 12. A witness provides a sworn statement in court about what they personally observed. This is an example of:

- [ ] Physical Evidence
- [x] Testimonial Evidence
- [ ] Documentary Evidence
- [ ] Circumstantial Evidence

> **Explanation:** Testimonial evidence is evidence given by a competent witness under oath or affirmation. It is what someone says in a legal proceeding, based on their direct knowledge or experience.

### 13. Which of the following is NOT one of the "Cardinal Points" of criminal investigation?

- [ ] What happened?
- [ ] Why did it happen?
- [x] What is the suspect's alibi?
- [ ] When did it happen?

> **Explanation:** The cardinal points are the essential questions an investigator must answer: Who, What, Where, When, Why, and How. "What is the suspect's alibi?" is a specific question that arises during the investigation of a particular suspect, not one of the foundational, high-level cardinal points that frame the entire inquiry.

### 14. The "Grid Search" method is most effective in which type of scenario?

- [ ] A single investigator in a small, circular room.
- [ ] Searching a large outdoor field for a small object.
- [x] A serious crime scene requiring a second, more thorough search.
- [ ] Searching a vehicle.

> **Explanation:** The grid method is a very thorough technique where an area is first searched using a strip pattern, and then searched again at a 90-degree angle to the first search. This double-search makes it ideal for complex scenes or when a high degree of thoroughness is required.

### 15. The lead investigator's initial walk-through of a crime scene is primarily for what purpose?

- [ ] To collect all visible evidence immediately.
- [x] To get an overview of the scene and formulate a plan for processing it.
- [ ] To identify and arrest any suspects still at the scene.
- [ ] To provide a statement to the media.

> **Explanation:** The initial walk-through is a crucial orientation step. It allows the lead investigator to observe the scene in its original, undisturbed state, identify potential evidence, note points of entry and exit, and develop a systematic plan for documentation and evidence collection.

### 16. A confession obtained through force, threat, or intimidation violates which of the three "I's" of investigation?

- [ ] Instrumentation, because it's unscientific.
- [x] Interrogation, because it's not obtained legally or ethically.
- [ ] Information, because the data is unreliable.
- [ ] All of the above.

> **Explanation:** While it touches on information quality, a coerced confession is fundamentally a violation of proper interrogation procedures. The goal of interrogation is to obtain a *voluntary* and truthful admission of guilt. Using illegal or unethical means like force negates the validity of the process and makes the resulting confession inadmissible.

### 17. The question "Who is the victim and who is the perpetrator?" falls under which cardinal point?

- [x] Who
- [ ] What
- [ ] Why
- [ ] How

> **Explanation:** The cardinal point "Who" is concerned with identifying every person involved in the crime, including the victim(s), perpetrator(s), and any witnesses.

### 18. An investigator finds a handwritten suicide note at a scene. This note is an example of:

- [ ] Physical Evidence
- [ ] Testimonial Evidence
- [x] Documentary Evidence
- [ ] Real Evidence

> **Explanation:** Documentary evidence is any evidence in the form of a document, whether written, printed, or otherwise recorded. A suicide note fits this category perfectly. While it is also a physical object, its primary value is the information it contains, making it documentary.

### 19. What is the main objective of a "crime scene sketch"?

- [ ] To serve as an artistic rendering of the crime.
- [x] To accurately record distances and relationships between objects and evidence.
- [ ] To be used as the sole piece of evidence in court.
- [ ] To create a quick reference for the media.

> **Explanation:** A crime scene sketch, whether rough or finished, is a technical drawing that complements photographs. Its primary purpose is to show the precise location of and relationship between evidence and key features of the scene, including accurate measurements that photos cannot convey.

### 20. When does the "chain of custody" for a piece of evidence officially begin?

- [ ] When the evidence is presented in court.
- [ ] When the evidence is being analyzed at the crime lab.
- [x] The moment an officer collects it at the crime scene.
- [ ] When the case file is opened.

> **Explanation:** The chain of custody starts at the point of collection. The collecting officer must immediately document their identity, the date, time, and location of the find, and assign an evidence number. This is the first link in the chain.

### 21. Which statement best defines "Criminal Investigation"?

- [ ] The process of arresting and detaining suspects.
- [ ] A public spectacle to deter future crime.
- [x] A systematic, legal process of identifying, collecting, and presenting evidence to determine the truth.
- [ ] The exclusive use of forensic science to solve crimes.

> **Explanation:** Criminal investigation is a comprehensive process. It is systematic (not random), legal (bound by rules of evidence and procedure), and its core purpose is to uncover the truth by gathering evidence to identify and prosecute the offender.

### 22. Which search pattern is a combination of the strip search and the grid search, often used for large outdoor areas?

- [ ] Spiral Method
- [x] Zone/Quadrant Method
- [ ] Wheel Method
- [ ] Link Method

> **Explanation:** The Zone or Quadrant method involves dividing the crime scene into smaller, more manageable sectors or zones. Each zone is then searched, often using another method like the strip or grid method within it. This is highly effective for large or complex areas.

### 23. A suspect's unique and unnecessary act committed during a crime, which is not required to complete the crime but satisfies a psychological need, is the:

- [ ] Modus Operandi
- [x] Criminal Signature
- [ ] Motive
- [ ] Intent

> **Explanation:** The signature is a behavioral distinction that helps to identify the work of a specific criminal. Unlike the MO, which is about *how* the criminal commits the crime (the mechanics), the signature is about *why* they do certain things that are personal to them (e.g., leaving a specific symbol, taking a particular souvenir).

### 24. What is the primary reason for immediately separating witnesses at a crime scene?

- [ ] To increase their anxiety and make them more talkative.
- [ ] To create a sense of competition among them.
- [x] To prevent them from influencing each other's testimony.
- [ ] To make it easier to provide them with refreshments.

> **Explanation:** Separating witnesses is a standard procedure to prevent them from cross-contaminating their recollections. If witnesses discuss the event among themselves, one person's account (whether accurate or not) can influence what others remember or report, tainting the integrity of their individual statements.

### 25. The 5 W's and 1 H are essential to an investigation. What does the 'H' stand for?

- [ ] Hearsay
- [ ] Hunch
- [x] How
- [ ] Honor

> **Explanation:** The six cardinal points of investigation are What, Where, When, Who, Why, and **How**. "How" refers to the method or manner in which the crime was committed.

### 26. Which of the following best describes the initial response phase of an investigation?

- [ ] The phase where the case is presented to the prosecutor.
- [x] The actions taken by the first police officers to arrive at the scene.
- [ ] The detailed analysis of evidence by the crime laboratory.
- [ ] The final verdict delivered by the court.

> **Explanation:** The initial response phase encompasses everything the first officer on the scene does, from the moment they are dispatched until the scene is handed over to the investigators-in-charge. This includes rendering aid, securing the scene, identifying witnesses, and documenting initial observations.

### 27. The process of questioning a person who is reluctant to provide information or who may be the suspect is known as:

- [ ] Interview
- [x] Interrogation
- [ ] Instrumentation
- [ ] Information Gathering

> **Explanation:** An interview is typically a non-accusatory conversation to gather information. An interrogation is more adversarial and is aimed at a suspect or someone believed to be withholding information, with the goal of eliciting the truth or a confession.

### 28. A surveillance video showing the suspect committing the crime is considered what kind of evidence?

- [ ] Testimonial and Physical
- [x] Documentary and Physical
- [ ] Only Physical
- [ ] Only Documentary

> **Explanation:** A video recording is a form of documentary evidence because it documents the event. It is also physical evidence because the recording medium (e.g., hard drive, memory card) is a tangible object that can be presented in court. It captures both images (documentary) and is itself a physical item.

### 29. The principle that "every contact leaves a trace" is fundamental to crime scene investigation and is known as:

- [ ] The Fruit of the Poisonous Tree Doctrine
- [x] Locard's Exchange Principle
- [ ] The Miranda Doctrine
- [ ] The Best Evidence Rule

> **Explanation:** Formulated by Dr. Edmond Locard, this principle states that whenever two objects come into contact, there is a mutual exchange of matter between them. This is the basis for collecting trace evidence like fibers, hair, soil, and gunshot residue.

### 30. An investigator is asking "Why was this particular person targeted?". This question addresses the:

- [ ] Modus Operandi
- [ ] Intent
- [x] Motive
- [ ] Opportunity

> **Explanation:** Motive is the reason or driving force behind the commission of a crime. It answers the "Why?" question. Intent is the state of mind to commit the act, but motive is the underlying reason for that intent (e.g., jealousy, revenge, financial gain).
