### 1. Which of the following best defines Penology?

- [ ] The study of criminal behavior and its causes.
- [x] The study of the punishment of crime and the management of correctional facilities.
- [ ] The sociological study of crime and its impact on society.
- [ ] The scientific analysis of physical evidence from a crime scene.

> Explanation: Penology, a sub-field of criminology, is specifically concerned with the theory and practice of punishment for criminal offenses and the management of prisons and other correctional institutions.

### 2. The classical theory of punishment that argues punishment should be equivalent to the harm done is known as:

- [ ] Deterrence
- [ ] Incapacitation
- [x] Retribution
- [ ] Rehabilitation

> Explanation: Retribution is the "eye for an eye" philosophy (lex talionis), where the primary goal of punishment is to make the offender suffer in a way that is proportional to the crime they committed.

### 3. What is the core principle of the Auburn (New York) System of corrections?

- [ ] Inmates were kept in solitary confinement at all times.
- [x] Inmates worked together in silence during the day and were isolated at night.
- [ ] The focus was primarily on religious and moral instruction.
- [ ] It emphasized vocational training over all other activities.

> Explanation: The Auburn System, also known as the "congregate system," was characterized by silent labor in workshops during the day and solitary confinement at night. This was a direct contrast to the Pennsylvania System's total isolation.

### 4. Which philosophy of correction focuses on changing the offender's behavior, skills, and mindset to prevent future crime?

- [ ] Retribution
- [ ] Incapacitation
- [x] Rehabilitation
- [ ] Deterrence

> Explanation: Rehabilitation aims to "correct" the offender through various programs like education, therapy, and vocational training, with the goal of successful reintegration into society.

### 5. In the context of Philippine corrections, what is the primary purpose of educational and vocational programs in prisons?

- [ ] To keep inmates busy and reduce idleness.
- [ ] To generate income for the correctional facility.
- [x] To provide inmates with skills for lawful employment upon release.
- [ ] To fulfill a basic requirement of international human rights standards.

> Explanation: The main goal of these programs is to equip inmates with marketable skills, thereby increasing their chances of finding legitimate employment after release and reducing the likelihood of recidivism.

### 6. The Therapeutic Community (TC) Model is best described as a correctional program that:

- [ ] Relies heavily on medication to manage inmate behavior.
- [x] Uses the community itself as the primary agent of change.
- [ ] Focuses solely on individual counseling with a psychiatrist.
- [ ] Segregates offenders based on the type of crime committed.

> Explanation: The TC Model is a self-help social learning treatment model. It uses the community, including peers and staff, to foster personal growth, responsibility, and positive behavioral change in a structured environment.

### 7. Republic Act No. 9344, or the "Juvenile Justice and Welfare Act," primarily promotes which approach to dealing with children in conflict with the law (CICL)?

- [ ] Punitive and retributive sanctions.
- [x] Restorative justice and rehabilitation.
- [ ] Strict incapacitation and isolation.
- [ ] Community service as the sole intervention.

> Explanation: R.A. 9344 emphasizes a restorative justice approach, focusing on the well-being of the child, diversion from the formal justice system, and rehabilitation rather than punishment.

### 8. The tendency of a convicted criminal to reoffend after being released is known as:

- [ ] Reformation
- [ ] Reintegration
- [x] Recidivism
- [ ] Retribution

> Explanation: Recidivism is a key concept in penology, measuring the rate at which former offenders commit new crimes. It is often used as an indicator of the effectiveness (or lack thereof) of correctional strategies.

### 9. Which of these is a critical component of successful reintegration for a former inmate?

- [ ] Severing all ties with their past community.
- [x] Securing stable housing and employment.
- [ ] Changing their legal identity.
- [ ] Paying a "debt to society" fee.

> Explanation: Successful reintegration, often called re-entry, heavily depends on practical support systems. Stable employment and a safe place to live are fundamental to preventing a return to criminal activity.

### 10. A modern correctional trend that focuses on repairing the harm caused by crime by involving victims, offenders, and the community is called:

- [ ] Punitive Justice
- [ ] Incapacitation Theory
- [x] Restorative Justice
- [ ] Scared Straight Programs

> Explanation: Restorative Justice moves beyond punishment to focus on healing and accountability. It involves dialogue and negotiation to repair the harm done to the victim and the community.

### 11. The Pennsylvania System, also known as the "separate system," was based on which core principle?

- [ ] Inmates learned a trade through factory-style work.
- [x] Total isolation and silence for reflection and penitence.
- [ ] Inmates governed themselves with minimal staff oversight.
- [ ] Physical punishment was the primary tool for discipline.

> Explanation: The Pennsylvania System at Eastern State Penitentiary was designed for complete solitary confinement, believing that isolation would lead inmates to penitence and reform.

### 12. Preventing crime by making potential offenders fearful of the consequences of their actions is the goal of:

- [ ] Rehabilitation
- [x] Deterrence
- [ ] Retribution
- [ ] Incapacitation

> Explanation: Deterrence theory posits that the threat of punishment (if it is certain, swift, and severe enough) will prevent people from committing crimes. It can be specific (for the individual offender) or general (for the public).

### 13. Which of the following is NOT considered a primary goal of punishment?

- [ ] Incapacitation
- [ ] Rehabilitation
- [ ] Deterrence
- [x] Retaliation

> Explanation: While related to retribution, "retaliation" implies personal revenge, which is not a formal, legal goal of a state-sanctioned justice system. Retribution is the formal, proportional consequence.

### 14. In the Philippines, the Board of Pardons and Parole is the agency responsible for:

- [ ] Managing the daily operations of all national prisons.
- [x] Granting parole and recommending executive clemency.
- [ ] Prosecuting criminal cases in court.
- [ ] Supervising juvenile detention centers.

> Explanation: The Board of Pardons and Parole (BPP) is tasked with evaluating inmates for release on parole and making recommendations to the President for pardons and other forms of executive clemency.

### 15. A vocational program teaching inmates computer repair is an example of which correctional philosophy?

- [ ] Retribution
- [x] Rehabilitation
- [ ] Incapacitation
- [ ] Just Deserts

> Explanation: This program is a clear example of rehabilitation, as it provides inmates with a specific, modern skill set to improve their employment prospects upon release.

### 16. What is a primary cause of recidivism?

- [ ] Excessive educational opportunities in prison.
- [ ] Overly lenient parole officers.
- [x] Social stigma and lack of employment opportunities post-release.
- [ ] Short prison sentences.

> Explanation: Ex-offenders face significant barriers to reintegration, including social stigma, discrimination from employers, and difficulty reconnecting with a positive social network, all of which contribute to reoffending.

### 17. The "Bahay Pag-asa" established under the Juvenile Justice and Welfare Act (R.A. 9344) serves as a:

- [ ] Maximum-security prison for juvenile offenders.
- [x] Youth care facility for residential care and rehabilitation.
- [ ] Halfway house for adult offenders.
- [ ] Vocational school for children of inmates.

> Explanation: "Bahay Pag-asa" centers are residential facilities designed to provide short-term care and rehabilitation for children in conflict with the law who are awaiting court disposition or serving a sentence.

### 18. This theory of punishment aims to make an offender incapable of committing further crimes, usually by imprisonment or execution.

- [ ] Deterrence
- [x] Incapacitation
- [ ] Rehabilitation
- [ ] Retribution

> Explanation: Incapacitation is the straightforward approach of removing an offender from society, thus physically preventing them from committing more crimes against the public.

### 19. Which historical figure is known for advocating for prison reform and the "Panopticon" design?

- [ ] Cesare Beccaria
- [x] Jeremy Bentham
- [ ] Cesare Lombroso
- [ ] John Howard

> Explanation: Jeremy Bentham, a philosopher and social reformer, developed the concept of the Panopticon, a circular prison design that would allow a single guard to observe all inmates without them knowing if they were being watched, thereby encouraging self-discipline.

### 20. A key feature of the Therapeutic Community (TC) model is the use of "peer confrontation." What is its purpose?

- [ ] To physically intimidate residents into compliance.
- [ ] To create a hierarchy within the community.
- [x] To hold residents accountable for their behavior in a constructive manner.
- [ ] To report rule violations to the prison administration for punishment.

> Explanation: In a TC, peer confrontation is a structured process where residents challenge each other's negative behaviors, attitudes, and thinking, forcing them to take responsibility in a supportive but firm environment.

### 21. What is the fundamental difference between probation and parole?

- [ ] Probation is for federal crimes, while parole is for state crimes.
- [x] Probation is an alternative to incarceration, while parole is early release from prison.
- [ ] Probation is supervised by the police, while parole is supervised by social workers.
- [ ] Probation is a right, while parole is a privilege.

> Explanation: A person is sentenced to probation INSTEAD of going to prison. A person is released on parole AFTER serving a portion of their prison sentence.

### 22. In the context of juvenile justice, what does "diversion" refer to?

- [ ] Transferring a juvenile to an adult court.
- [ ] Sending a juvenile to a different foster home.
- [x] Disposing of a case without resorting to formal court proceedings.
- [ ] Allowing a juvenile to change their plea.

> Explanation: Diversion aims to keep CICL out of the formal justice system by using alternative programs like counseling, community service, or restorative justice conferences.

### 23. The "Just Deserts" model of punishment is most closely aligned with which theory?

- [ ] Rehabilitation
- [x] Retribution
- [ ] Deterrence
- [ ] Incapacitation

> Explanation: "Just Deserts" is a modern form of retribution, emphasizing that the punishment should be directly proportional to the seriousness of the offense. Offenders get what they "justly deserve."

### 24. Which of these is NOT a form of executive clemency in the Philippines?

- [ ] Pardon (Absolute or Conditional)
- [ ] Commutation of Sentence
- [x] Probation
- [ ] Reprieve

> Explanation: Probation is a disposition of the court and is not a form of executive clemency, which is granted by the President. Executive clemency includes pardon, amnesty, reprieve, and commutation of sentence.

### 25. The shift from a purely punitive model to a rehabilitative model of corrections is often called the:

- [ ] Retributive Movement
- [x] Reformatory Movement
- [ ] Auburn Movement
- [ ] Classical Movement

> Explanation: The Reformatory Movement, which gained momentum in the late 19th century, was characterized by the introduction of parole, individualized treatment, and educational/vocational programs, marking a significant shift towards rehabilitation.

### 26. Which factor is considered a "static" risk factor for recidivism?

- [ ] Substance abuse problem
- [x] Offender's criminal history
- [ ] Anti-social peer group
- [ ] Lack of employment

> Explanation: Static risk factors are historical and cannot be changed, such as an offender's age at first offense or their total number of prior convictions. Dynamic risk factors (like employment or substance abuse) can be changed through intervention.

### 27. What is the primary purpose of a halfway house in the reintegration process?

- [ ] To serve as a long-term residence for ex-offenders.
- [x] To provide a transitional living arrangement with supervision and support.
- [ ] To act as a community-based prison.
- [ ] To function as a job placement agency.

> Explanation: A halfway house provides a bridge between the structured environment of a prison and the freedom of the community, offering a supervised setting where ex-offenders can gradually adjust to life outside.

### 28. The concept of "penal populism" refers to:

- [ ] The belief that all offenders can be rehabilitated.
- [x] Tough-on-crime policies pursued by politicians for public approval.
- [ ] A correctional model managed by the inmates themselves.
- [ ] A focus on community-based sanctions over imprisonment.

> Explanation: Penal populism describes the trend where politicians enact increasingly punitive criminal justice policies to win votes, often without regard for their effectiveness or cost.

### 29. According to the Bureau of Jail Management and Penology (BJMP), what is a primary component of their welfare and development programs for inmates?

- [ ] International travel opportunities.
- [ ] Unrestricted internet access.
- [x] Livelihood projects and skills training.
- [ ] Access to social media.

> Explanation: The BJMP focuses on inmate welfare and development through programs like livelihood projects (e.g., handicrafts), skills training, education, and health services to prepare them for reintegration.

### 30. A philosophy that views the prison itself as a 'community of learners' where staff and inmates work together to foster change is central to what model?

- [ ] The Medical Model
- [x] The Therapeutic Community Model
- [ ] The Custodial Model
- [ ] The Retributive Model

> Explanation: The Therapeutic Community model is unique in its view of the entire prison/ward environment as a learning tool where every interaction and relationship is an opportunity for therapeutic intervention and personal growth.
